AI TUTORS VS. STUBBORN MYTHS

Can a Conversation Change Your Mind?

Misconceptions in psychology and education persist despite clear contradictory evidence, resisting traditional correction methods. This study investigates whether personalised AI dialogue can effectively correct these stubborn beliefs where textbook refutations have failed.

Published in Computers in Human Behavior

THE CHALLENGE: Why Myths Stick

Despite extensive research debunking them, these misconceptions remain widespread amongst teachers, students, and the general public. They feel intuitively correct and align with our everyday experiences, making them remarkably resistant to correction.

We tested 16 psychology misconceptions (N=375):

1 Individuals learn best when information is presented in their preferred learning style, such as visual, auditory, or kinaesthetic. +

THE EXPERIMENT (N=375)

Participants holding strong psychology misconceptions were randomly assigned to one of three interventions:

🤖

Personalised AI Tutor

Three-round conversation with Claude 3.5 Sonnet. Active, reasoning-based dialogue targeting participant's specific misconception and beliefs.

N = 125
See example conversation below
📖

Textbook Refutation

Evidence-based passage addressing the misconception. Passive reading of factual, corrective information in textbook style.

N = 125
💬

Neutral AI Chat

Control condition. Interactive conversation on unrelated topics (healthcare, pets, firefighters) with same format but different content.

N = 125

Example from Condition 1: Personalised AI Tutor

Sample conversation addressing: "People learn best when information is presented in their preferred learning style"

▼
👤 Participant (Initial Belief: 100/100)
I think this is true as I have a background in teaching and have recognised the different ways students learn. There seems to be a variance of ways that knowledge can be kept with them versus the knowledge they forgot / misunderstood. I have worked alongside all ages and it is dependent on how the information is presented in different ways, people react differently to this and therefore I think this reaction can impact learning.
🤖 AI Tutor
Thank you for sharing your perspective on learning styles. It's clear that your background in teaching has given you valuable insights into how students engage with information. Your observations about students' different reactions to various presentation methods are certainly valid and important.

While it's true that people may have preferences for how information is presented, recent research in cognitive psychology and education science has challenged the idea that matching teaching to preferred learning styles significantly improves learning outcomes. For instance, a comprehensive review by Pashler et al. (2008) found no strong evidence to support the idea that presenting information in a person's preferred learning style leads to better learning. In fact, they suggested that the most effective teaching methods often depend more on the content being taught, the prior knowledge, and the social context of learning rather than individual learning style preferences.
👤 Participant
I think this is an interesting theory. I would definitely be intrigued by that review and the chosen group for the review. I think it could be seen as context can vary. For example, if it is content vs delivery method that changes the way we interpret and learn? I would be inclined to say that unless the same study is conducted on the same people over a specific period of time on a topic they have 0 initial knowledge on then we cannot yet determine whether they are learning because of their preferred method or because of what is being taught.
🤖 AI Tutor
You raise some excellent points, and I appreciate your thoughtful response. Your critical approach to evaluating research findings is commendable. You're absolutely right to question the methodology; these factors are crucial for assessing the validity and applicability of research results. Your suggestion about conducting a more controlled study over time with participants who have no initial knowledge of the subject is insightful.

However, it's worth noting that numerous studies have attempted to address these variables. For example, Kratzig and Arbuthnott (2006) tested students across multiple learning domains and found little correlation between learning style preferences and actual learning performance. This type of research addresses precisely the issue you raised: the need for studying how students learn that might not fit neatly into the traditional learning styles categories.

THE RESULTS: Belief Change Over Time

Immediate Impact

AI Dialogue produced the largest immediate reduction in belief (39 points vs 31 points for Textbook), with both significantly outperforming the control.

Long-term Convergence

By 2 months, AI and Textbook conditions converged. The AI advantage fades without reinforcement, suggesting the need for spaced follow-up.

THE HIDDEN ADVANTAGE: Engagement & Confidence

Beyond belief change, the AI dialogue intervention generated significantly higher participant engagement and confidence compared to static text reading.

💡 KEY TAKEAWAY FOR ED-TECH & L&D

Personalised AI dialogue is a powerful accelerator for breaking down stubborn myths because it forces active reasoning and creates cognitive conflict that's hard to dismiss. However, it's not a "one-and-done" magic bullet. Lasting change requires spaced reinforcement to sustain the initial breakthrough. Future applications should integrate AI tutoring into structured educational programmes with follow-up sessions to maintain the advantages of personalised dialogue.
