AI TUTORS VS. STUBBORN MYTHS

Can a conversation change your mind?

Misconceptions in psychology and education persist even when students are exposed to accurate information. This preregistered experiment compares a personalised AI tutor with a text-based refutation and a neutral AI chat to see which approach most effectively shifts belief in 16 common myths.

Participants N = 375
Myths tested 16
Interventions 3
Status Published in Computers in Human Behavior
View published article →

THE CHALLENGE

Why myths stick

Neuromyths and psychological misconceptions are intuitive, memorable, and reinforced by everyday experience. Even when students see accurate information, the myth often “feels” right and snaps back into place.

This study examines whether a short, targeted conversation with an AI tutor can disrupt that snap-back effect more effectively than a traditional myth-busting passage or a neutral chat that avoids the misconception entirely.

THE EXPERIMENT

Three brief interventions

Participants rated their belief in 16 myths, then completed one of three brief interventions targeting their strongest myth. Afterwards, they rated their belief again and completed follow-up measures of engagement and confidence.

Personalised AI Tutor

A short, tailored conversation where an AI tutor explicitly names the myth, explains why it is inaccurate, and offers a plausible, evidence-based alternative. The tutor asks follow-up questions so the participant actively works with the new explanation.

  • Targets the participant’s own words and reasons
  • Uses questions to prompt reflection and elaboration
  • Draws on cognitive and learning science principles

Textbook-Style Refutation

A concise, standardised passage that names the myth, states that it is false, and provides a clear corrective explanation. The text is matched in length and content coverage to typical myth-busting materials.

  • Same passage for all participants with that myth
  • Explains why the myth is wrong and what is accurate
  • No personalisation or questioning

Neutral AI Conversation

A friendly, off-topic chat that avoids the misconception entirely (e.g., cats vs dogs). This controls for the experience of chatting with an AI without providing corrective information.

  • Similar length and tone to the tutor condition
  • No myth mentioned, no corrective content
  • Helps isolate the effect of targeted correction

RESULTS

Belief change over time

The figure below shows belief in the target myth (0–100 scale) before and after the intervention, and at follow-up. All three interventions reduced belief, but the AI tutor produced the largest and most durable shift.

AI Tutor: largest immediate drop and strongest maintenance at follow-up.

Text-based refutation: clear initial shift, but slightly more rebound.

Neutral chat: small changes, consistent with regression to the mean and repeated ratings.

BEYOND BELIEFS

Engagement & confidence

Engagement

Participants in both AI chat conditions reported higher engagement and enjoyment than those who read the text passage. The personalised tutor was especially likely to be described as “interesting”, “helpful”, and “easy to follow”.

Confidence in Understanding

After the AI tutor conversation, participants felt more confident explaining why the myth was inaccurate and what the accurate explanation was. Confidence gains were smaller in the text condition and minimal in the neutral chat condition.

MYTHS TESTED

Optional deep dive

The study targeted 16 myths spanning brain, learning, memory, perception, and mental health. Below are a few examples. You can expand the full list if you’d like to see all items.

  • “People only use 10% of their brain.”
  • “Learning styles (visual, auditory, kinaesthetic) should guide teaching.”
  • “Exposing babies to classical music boosts their intelligence.”
1 People only use 10% of their brain. +

A classic neuromyth suggesting most of the brain lies dormant. The study contrasted this with evidence from neuroimaging and brain injury.

2 Learning styles should drive instruction. +

The popular idea that matching teaching to “visual”, “auditory” or “kinaesthetic” learners boosts learning, despite very limited empirical support.

3 Exposing babies to classical music boosts intelligence. +

A developmental myth suggesting that early exposure to specific music will substantially raise IQ, beyond general benefits of a rich environment.

RIGOUR & OPEN SCIENCE

How the study was run

Preregistered & powered

The design and confirmatory analyses were preregistered, with a sample size chosen to detect practically meaningful differences between interventions.

Transparent analyses

Data cleaning and analysis were conducted in R, with code and analysis decisions documented for reproducibility.

Open materials

Study materials, myth wordings, and intervention prompts are shared to support replication and reuse in teaching and research.

Example: Personalised AI tutor correcting a neuromyth

This is a shortened example from the Personalised AI Tutor condition, showing how the tutor names the myth, explains why it is inaccurate, and offers an alternative explanation.

I’ve always heard that people only use 10% of their brain. Is that true?
That’s a really common idea, but it’s actually a myth. Would you like me to walk you through why scientists don’t think the 10% claim is accurate?
Yes, I’d like to understand why it’s wrong.
Great. First, brain scans show that we use most of our brain over the course of a day. Different areas become active for different tasks, even when we’re resting or daydreaming. If 90% of the brain were unused, we wouldn’t see that.
Second, if most of the brain were unnecessary, damage to many areas shouldn’t matter – but in reality, even small injuries can have serious effects on language, movement, or memory.
So the 10% idea comes more from the way people talk about potential than from actual science?
Exactly. It’s often used as a metaphor for “untapped potential”, but as a scientific claim it isn’t accurate. A better way to think about it is that the brain is highly efficient and plastic – it can reorganise and strengthen connections with practice.
