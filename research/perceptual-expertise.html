PhD Research

Perceptual Expertise in High-Stakes Decisions

What separates those who reach exceptional performance from those who do not?

Domain Fingerprint Examination
Methods Mixed-Methods
Studies 3 Empirical Chapters
Scroll to explore
01

The Stakes

Fingerprint examination plays a critical role in forensic identification, yet relies heavily on subjective comparisons by expert examiners. Mistakes can have severe consequences—wrongful convictions or guilty parties going free.

Despite fingerprints serving as identification evidence for over a century, scientific bodies have called for more research on the validity and reliability of fingerprint analysis. Understanding the psychological mechanisms underlying expertise is essential for ensuring accuracy in this high-stakes domain.

Why This Matters

Examiners spend hours comparing prints from crime scenes to potential matches. Their judgments directly impact justice outcomes. Yet the cognitive processes underlying their expertise have remained largely unexamined.

02

The Research Program

This PhD research used fingerprint identification as an ecological testbed to explore the complex knowledge structures, cognitive processes, and personal faculties that lead to exceptional performance. Across three studies, I employed a mixed-methods approach to reverse engineer expertise.

03

How Experts Think

Using think-aloud protocols with 44 fingerprint examiners and 44 novices, we discovered that expertise is not just about knowing more—it's about thinking differently.

1

Attentional Focus

Experts focused on local ridge characteristics and minute details, while novices concentrated on global features.

2

Adaptive Strategy

Experts demonstrated adaptability in dynamically planning their systematic approach to each unique print.

3

Domain Knowledge

Experts integrated specialised knowledge of causal biological and mechanical factors affecting prints.

4

Critical Evaluation

Experts displayed metacognition, carefully weighing the reliability of each identifying feature before concluding.

"Becoming an expert is not just about knowing more; it is about thinking differently. This qualitative shift in cognition redefines the way experts engage with their domain."

05

How Experts Remember

Superior memory may be integral to attaining and maintaining proficiency in fingerprint examination. Across two experiments, we tested whether expertise enhances visual short-term memory for domain-specific stimuli.

Experiment 1

Experts showed substantially higher memory performance compared to novices for fingerprints from their domain of expertise.

44 experts vs 44 novices

Experiment 2

While both groups benefited from distinctive prints, experts still outperformed novices especially for nondistinctive prints—the harder cases.

Distinctiveness manipulation

What This Means

Beyond stimulus qualities, expertise itself enhances short-term memory, likely through more effective organisational processing and sensitivity to meaningful patterns. This suggests training to improve memory for diverse fingerprints could practically boost examiner performance.

06

What Predicts Expertise

If certain inherent traits predict expertise, screening based on these factors may help identify candidates most likely to excel. We compared 42 fingerprint experts with 42 novices across cognitive tasks and personality measures.

Cognitive Tasks

  • Visual search
  • Mental rotation
  • Inhibition
  • Intelligence measures

Personality Measures

  • Big Five traits
  • Risk propensity
  • Intolerance of uncertainty
  • Resilience
Strongest Predictors Visual Search & Mental Rotation

However, the predictive power of these traits was subtle and was overshadowed by domain-specific perceptual skills (measured by the Fingerprint Expertise Quotient). Extensive experience in the field emerged as the dominant force.

The Takeaway

Expertise in fingerprint identification demands a blend of specialised experience and effective training, while also benefiting from certain innate abilities and traits.

Given the gravity of fingerprint analysis for security, justice, and liberty, applying these insights to strengthen training and practice is an ethical imperative. By refining selection and instruction grounded in science, we can nurture and empower experts to reach their full potential.

08

Implications

Training Design

Balance intuitive pattern recognition with analytical reasoning. Emphasise diverse exposure to varied exemplars.

Memory Enhancement

Training programs could focus on improving memory for diverse fingerprints to boost practical performance.

Selection Criteria

Consider visual search and spatial abilities alongside domain-specific testing when recruiting trainees.

Documentation

Implement more detailed documentation practices and incorporate falsification practices in evidence evaluation.
